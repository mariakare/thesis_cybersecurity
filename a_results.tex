\section{Evaluation Overview}
% Briefly explain models, splits, metrics and how to read the results.




This chapter presents the experimental results for the evaluated unsupervised ano\-maly detection models on the constructed reconnaissance detection datasets. The goal is to assess whether models trained only on benign conntrack-derived traffic can detect injected reconnaissance activity in real OT background traffic. The evaluated configurations vary in model family, feature set, aggregation window length, anomaly threshold and model-specific parameters, as described in Section~\ref{sec:experimental_configuration}.

The evaluation is organized in three steps. First, the customer 1 validation split is used to rank feature sets and model configurations. Second, the selected configurations are evaluated on the separate customer 1 test split to assess robustness across a different benign traffic period. Finally, the same selected configurations are evaluated on customer 2 as a cross-environment check.


Unless stated otherwise, the tables in this chapter report configurations selected according to validation performance, followed by their corresponding test-set performance.  The reported overall scores aggregate all evaluated reconnaissance scenarios, ranging from the more aggressive T5/T4 scans to the slower T1/T0 scans. Differences between validation and test results are interpreted as an indication of robustness and sensitivity to the underlying benign traffic period.

The goal of this selection was not only to find the highest individual validation score, but to identify feature sets that performed well across multiple reconnaissance scenarios. Feature sets that were designed for one specific attack type were therefore interpreted with caution, even when they achieved strong results on that specific attack. Instead, preference was given to feature sets with broader coverage across the evaluated attacks. To summarize the validation results across feature sets, a compact selection score is used. This score is the average of the mean validation range F1 and the mean validation window-level F1. Table~\ref{tab:validation_feature_set_ranking} shows the resulting ranking. 


The number of configurations is not identical for all feature sets. The general-purpose feature sets were included in the full K-Means and autoencoder validation sweeps. In contrast, \feat{adam\_entropy} was evaluated as an ADAM-inspired k-NN baseline and therefore has fewer configurations. It will be further discussed in Section~\ref{sec:overall_model_performance}. Similarly, \feat{horizontal\_scan\_clean} and \feat{ablation\_asymmetry\_only} were dropped earlier based on their weaker validation behavior, so their scores are based on a smaller set of configurations. 

The two strongest feature sets are \feat{golden\_minimal} and \feat{omni\_core\_clean}, which both achieve the highest combined validation scores. Since \feat{golden\_minimal} is a subset of \feat{omni\_core\_clean}, its stronger validation score suggests that the additional features in \feat{omni\_core\_clean} do not necessarily improve detection performance.
\feat{low\_slow\_clean} is also retained because it is the next strongest general-purpose feature set and is relevant for slower reconnaissance behaviour. Although \feat{T2\_stealth\_scan} achieves competitive scores in some cases, it is more closely tied to a specific attack type and is therefore not selected.

The three highest-scoring general feature sets thus are \feat{golden\_minimal}, \feat{omni\_core\_clean}, and \feat{low\_slow\_clean}. These are therefore retained for the final cross-model comparison and for the reduced k-NN evaluation.

\subsection{Validation Ranking Results}
The next step is to compare the best-performing configurations across the evaluated model families. Since many model, feature-set, window-size and threshold combinations were evaluated, the focus lies on the strongest configurations and on the main patterns that emerge from the validation results. The full top-ranked validation configurations are provided in Appendix~\ref{tab:appendix_top_val_f1} and Appendix~\ref{tab:appendix_top_val_rf1}, where the models are ranked separately by window-level F1 and range-level F1. The appendix rankings show that the highest window-level F1 scores are dominated by CPS-GUARD configurations with 60-second windows. These configurations achieve very high window-level F1 and also strong range-level scores. In contrast, several of the highest range-F1 configurations use 5-minute windows and obtain perfect or near-perfect range precision with high range recall.

\subsection{Interpreting Window-Level and Range-Level Metrics}
Both window-level F1 and range-level F1 are used because they capture different aspects of the detection task. Window-level F1 evaluates each source-host window as a separate prediction, and therefore measures how accurately the model labels individual time windows. Range-level F1 instead groups consecutive anomalous windows into alarm ranges and compares these with the true reconnaissance intervals. This gives a more operational view of the detector, since an analyst would usually investigate an alarm period rather than isolated individual windows.

The two metrics can lead to different conclusions. A model may achieve a high range-level score even when it only detects part of an attack interval, as long as at least one alarm range overlaps the true attack range. Conversely, a model may detect many attack windows but still obtain a lower range-level score if its false positives are spread over many separate alarm ranges. Larger aggregation windows and range-level evaluation also reduce the number of evaluation units, so a single missed attack range or false alarm range can have a strong effect on the final score.

The rankings also show why F1 should not be interpreted in isolation. Several configurations achieve similar F1 or range F1 values while making different trade-offs between precision and recall. In particular, a high range F1 can be obtained either by detecting almost all attack ranges with some additional false alarm ranges, or by producing very few false alarm ranges while missing one or more attack ranges. Although both types of error are relevant, missed attack ranges are more problematic in the context of early reconnaissance detection. A small number of additional false alarm ranges can still be inspected by an analyst, whereas a false negative means that an entire reconnaissance interval may remain unnoticed. Therefore, when configurations have comparable F1 scores, preference is given to models with no, or fewer, range false negatives. However, false positives remain important for operational usability, so the main ranking still uses F1 rather than a recall-weighted \(F_{\beta}\)-score.

\subsection{Computational Cost and Practical Trade-offs}
\label{sec:computational_tradeoffs}

Besides detection performance, the models also differ in computational cost. All evaluated model families are feasible for real-time use in the sense that their evaluation time remains below the smallest evaluated window length of 60 seconds. However, their efficiency differs substantially. 

K-Means is the fastest model family, because each new window only has to be compared with a small number of centroids. CPS-GUARD is more expensive because each window must pass through the autoencoder, but inference still has a fixed cost once the model is trained. k-NN is the slowest option, since each evaluated window must be compared with the stored benign reference windows to compute nearest-neighbour distances. This cost becomes especially relevant for short windows, where both the number of reference windows and the number of evaluated windows are larger.

These results suggest that the main issue is not only the choice of model family, but also the environment-specific calibration of the anomaly threshold. In unsupervised anomaly detection, the anomaly score distribution depends on the benign traffic of the target environment. If the benign traffic in customer 2 is more variable, or contains behaviour that resembles reconnaissance indicators, then a threshold that is appropriate for customer 1 can become too strict or too permissive for customer 2. This can lead either to missed attacks or to many false positives.

A second analysis therefore allows the threshold or contamination value to be selected using the customer 2 results. For CPS-GUARD, K-Means and k-NN, the feature set is also allowed to vary between \feat{golden\_minimal} and \feat{omni\_core\_clean}. This comparison is shown in Table~\ref{tab:customer2_tuned_configs}. Since \feat{golden\_minimal} is a subset of \feat{omni\_core\_clean}, this also tests whether the additional features in \feat{omni\_core\_clean} are useful in the second environment.

This seems to improves some results but only to a limited degree. For CPS-GUARD, selecting for window-level F1 increases recall substantially, but precision remains low and the range-level F1 stays close to zero. This means that many attack windows are detected, but the predicted alarm ranges are dominated by false positives. The same pattern is visible for the F1-selected $k$-NN configuration. K-Means obtains its best range-level result with \feat{omni\_core\_clean}, suggesting that the additional features can help in this environment, but its overall detection quality is still weak. The ADAM-inspired baseline remains the most precise configuration, but its recall is low, so it detects only part of the attack activity.

The detailed per-attack results in Appendix~\ref{app:customer_2_per_attack} confirm this pattern. The strongest customer 2 results are mainly driven by the T5/T4/T3 scenario, while the stealthier and slower scenarios remain difficult.  Under the original injected-attack-only labels, this appears as a large false-positive problem. However, this interpretation assumes that all non-injected customer traffic is benign.

To verify this assumption, the top flagged anomalies in customer 2 were investigated. This analysis showed that at least part of the detected traffic corresponds to real suspicious activity in the customer environment. Specifically, the customer had an Internet exposed service on port 86, and a large number of external source IPs were observed connecting to this port. This traffic corresponded to a brute-force, password guessing event against the exposed service.

The timing of this activity is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:customer2_port86_timeline}. The port-86 traffic appears as a concentrated burst around the end of March, and the fixed customer 2 test interval overlaps with most of this activity. This explains the exceptionally low precision scores of the models evaluated on this test data, with the high FP rate being contributed to by this real attack activity. This shows that the models can detect relevant suspicious behaviour in the operational background traffic beyond reconnaissance.

This environment dependence is not unexpected in OT networks, where communication patterns are very specific to the devices, protocls, maintainance routines and general operation of the site. A feature set or threshold that separates reconnaissance activity clearly from benign traffic in one environment may overlap much more with benign behaviour in another. This suggests that future evaluations should include more environments during model selection, rather than selecting configurations only on a single customer. The best configuration may not be the one that achieves the highest score on one environment, but the one that provides the best trade-off across several environments.

From an operational perspective, the selected CPS-GUARD configuration is the most suitable candidate among the evaluated models. It preforms well across both evaluation metrics, meaning that it not only identifies many malicious windows but also produces alarm ranges that correspond well to the injected reconnaissance intervals. 

This shows why range-level evaluation is important for practical use. A detector that achieves high window-level recall can still be difficult to use if its false positives are spread over many separate alarm ranges. For K-Means and k-NN, while they detect many attack windows, they produce lower range precision. In a SOC setting, this would increase the number of alarm periods that an analyst has to inspect. Therefore, the most useful configuration is not necessarily the one with the highest window-level recall, but the one that provides a manageable number of meaningful alarm ranges.
